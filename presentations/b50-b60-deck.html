What to Learn from
B50 Before B60

B50 is a major achievement. B60 should wait for what national experience can teach.

Dr. Toronata Tambun
Ambassador for the En-ROADS Climate Simulation
Climate Interactive and MIT Sustainability Initiative
© 2026 Dr. Toronata Tambun
01  ·  Introduction

This is not an argument against B60. It is a question about what B50 must first reveal.

The frame matters: the next percentage should not become a symbol of progress before the present blend has produced sustained national experience.

Indonesia reached B50 after nearly two decades of changes in fuel standards, production capacity, distribution and engine testing. The programme had barely begun its national operating life when President Prabowo Subianto asked when Indonesia could proceed to B60.

"What happens when the blend rises above B50?"

— A friend's question, after hearing the exchange about B60
The Frame

The next percentage should not become a symbol of progress before the present blend has produced sustained national experience. B50 is the test that earns B60 — or delays it.

02  ·  Research Gap

Public discussion moves among four terms as though they describe the same achievement.

Swasembada, Ketahanan, Kemandirian, Kedaulatan — each word names a different claim. Conflating them obscures how much B50 has actually achieved, and how much remains to be established.

Core Gap

Indonesia reports B50 as an energy milestone, but the assessment typically covers only visible outputs — diesel imports avoided and foreign exchange retained. Net methanol dependence, fiscal durability, regional fuel quality, engine reliability and the consequences for land and people are not yet reported together. A national milestone is credible only when output, cost, reliability and consequences are counted in the same account.

B50 creates a real national gain — but the gain must be measured across the whole system, not only at the blending terminal.

03  ·  Framework

Four Words, Four Claims — A Framework for Assessment

Government Regulation No. 40/2025 defines these four terms. Each measures a different dimension of what Indonesia's biodiesel programme is achieving.

I
Swasembada
Supply Adequacy

Can domestic production meet domestic demand? This is the most visible metric — the one most often reported. B50 addresses it directly by substituting domestic FAME for imported petroleum diesel.

II
Ketahanan
System Reliability

Can energy remain available, accessible and affordable over time? Reliability requires more than supply — it requires consistent quality, regional reach, and pricing that fishermen, farmers and transport operators can sustain.

III
Kemandirian
Critical Dependence

Can the system rely on domestic resources, technology and components? FAME production still uses imported methanol. Planned methanol plants in Bojonegoro and East Kalimantan would bring more of the supply chain inside Indonesia.

IV
Kedaulatan
Authority and Control

Can the state and nation determine energy policy in pursuit of security and independence? Sovereignty requires that the policy benefits and burdens are distributed justly — including for Indigenous peoples and customary communities whose land makes the programme possible.

04  ·  Details

The Long Road to B50 — and What the Supply Chain Reveals

Indonesia did not arrive at B50 through one declaration. Each stage accumulated standards, infrastructure and experience. The supply chain shows where that experience is still incomplete.

2008
B2.5 — The programme begins

A low blend introduces the supply chain to FAME. Infrastructure, standards and operator practice begin to accumulate.

2020
B30 — Large-scale national operation

B30 becomes normal across the fleet. Engine and distribution experience at this blend is well established.

2023
B35 — The vehicle fleet adapts again

Higher FAME content enters national use. Filterability and storage management requirements intensify.

2025
B40 — Higher FAME in national use

The blend rises further. Engineering evidence from this stage informs what B50 will demand of the system.

2026
B50 — The current national test

50% FAME in the blend. The supply chain, engine fleet, and institutional systems are now being tested at national scale for the first time at this concentration.

⚠ The Supply Chain Is the Policy

A blending mandate becomes real only when the same fuel quality reaches engines across the archipelago. Water enters storage. Blending is delayed. Handling weakens quality. Remote delivery costs rise. Fuel arrives unevenly. Maintenance burdens appear. Each handoff from FAME plant → port → ship → blending terminal → storage → remote depot → engine is a point where the policy can fail.

05  ·  Evidence

Engineering Evidence from B30 and B40

Higher blends must be judged through filterability and real operating experience. Controlled tests establish feasibility. Long operation reveals the adaptations required for reliability.

EVIDENCE A — Filterability Study
B30–B40 Pressure Drop Findings

Pramudito et al. (2023) examined how FAME concentration, temperature and monoglyceride content affected filterability. Under some conditions, B40 produced a greater pressure difference across the filter than B30 — a measurable engineering constraint that grows with blend concentration.

DOI: 10.14710/ijred.2023.52801

EVIDENCE B — Offshore Operating Case
West Seno Bangka B30 Experience

Ariyanto (2023) documents engine-start failures, unplanned stoppages and filter blockage at an offshore installation operating on B30. Changes in fuel treatment improved filterability and restored equipment reliability — confirming that adaptation is both necessary and achievable, but not automatic.

EVIDENCE C — Import Substitution Balance
Net Independence After Full Accounting

Lower diesel imports do not complete the calculation. FAME production still uses imported methanol during transesterification. Lower diesel imports and higher biodiesel support belong in the same fiscal account. Net independence is measured after imports, incentives and delivery costs are counted together.

EVIDENCE D — HVO Scale Constraint
Pertamina Green Refinery Cilacap

HVO (hydrotreated vegetable oil) has properties closer to petroleum diesel. Pertamina's Green Refinery Cilacap is planned at 3,000 barrels per day, expanding to 6,000 bpd — an emerging capability, but still small beside national diesel demand. Cilacap cannot yet be assumed to provide the technical route from B50 to B60.

06  ·  Implications

What Is Measured and What Is Missing

A national milestone is credible only when output, cost, reliability and consequences are reported together. The current B50 assessment covers less than half of what matters.

⚠Engine reliability and maintenance in national use

The Noble Option

Indonesia can advance its energy programme — reducing diesel imports, building domestic methanol capacity, improving supply reliability, developing HVO capability — while protecting: no new natural-forest or peat conversion, every additional tonne of feedstock traced, customary communities central to decisions affecting their land and livelihoods. The stronger legacy is not only a higher blend, but a fuel system whose benefits and burdens are shared justly.

07  ·  Conclusion

Six Conditions Before B60

B50 should generate the evidence that determines the next step. These six conditions define what that evidence must show.

1

Publish net import savings after imported methanol is counted.

2

Report the levy support and the cost of keeping blended fuel affordable.

3

Measure regional availability and fuel quality across the archipelago.

4

Track engine reliability, filterability and maintenance in national use.

5

Verify additional feedstock without new forest or peat conversion.

6

Give Indigenous peoples and customary communities authority in decisions affecting their land.

Closing

Indonesia reached B50 through successive stages rather than one sudden declaration, and B60 should inherit the same patience. B50 will carry the meaning of independence only when its benefits can also be felt by smallholders, plantation workers, fishing families, Indigenous communities and households still struggling to afford energy and food. They are the people Indonesia's independence was meant to serve.
