What the classroom
never received

Indonesia keeps changing what teachers are told to deliver while neglecting the earlier formation of attention, memory, number sense and self-command.

Dr. Toronata Tambun
Director, Yayasan Mens et Manus
© 2026 Dr. Toronata Tambun
01 — Introduction

The classroom is asked to repair habits formed before school.

A SCQA reading of the core argument.

Situation

Children enter school with attention, patience, recall and self-command already shaped by family routines and early exposure.

Complication

Phones, and later AI, make cognitive outsourcing routine before schools and universities define what must remain human work.

Question

Where should Indonesia begin if stronger classrooms are the goal?

Answer

Begin with formation before curriculum launch: early childhood, parent guidance, primary-school habits, teacher preparation and continuity.

© 2026 Dr. Toronata Tambun
02 — Gap

The central problem is not inside the classroom.

The habits schools are asked to build have already been formed — or deformed — before any curriculum reaches the child.

Central Gap

"A classroom does not receive a blank slate." Weakness visible in secondary school or university has usually been built across earlier years — in family routines, in screen exposure, in the absence of anyone naming attention, patience and number sense as things worth protecting.

Reform efforts in Indonesia consistently begin at the curriculum document, reaching teachers as new language and new demands. What they rarely address is the period before formal instruction — the years when the habits that schooling depends on are either formed or foreclosed. Every new curriculum inherits the consequences of that earlier neglect.

AI accelerates the exposure of this gap. A student who has never built sustained attention, recall or number sense does not become a more capable learner when a language model is placed in front of them. The tool surfaces the deficit; it does not repair it.

03 — Framework

Three arguments that hold the piece together.

The column builds its case through three sequential claims, each exposing a different layer of the same failure.

01
Formation precedes school

Attention, patience, recall and self-command are shaped before the classroom receives the child. The site of formation is family and early exposure — not curriculum.

02
Reform substitutes documents for absorption

Competency language, Curriculum 2013 and Kurikulum Merdeka followed each other. The pattern is faster redesign at the top than change inside classroom habit. The weakest point is absorption, not the document.

03
AI exposes weak pedagogy at both ends

A student can produce plausible text without owning the reasoning behind it. Each field — law, engineering, medicine, design, history — must define what students still need to do themselves.

04 — Details

The reform pattern in closer view.

Three pressure points that together explain why curriculum redesign has not reached classroom habit.

Measurement arrived without diagnosis

PISA 2022 recorded 366 in mathematics, 383 in science and 359 in reading. The scores arrived and were reported. What did not follow was a sustained diagnosis of why — tracing the deficit back to pre-school formation, family routines and the years before any teacher could intervene. The score became the story; the cause did not.

Curriculum sequence without absorption

Competency language, school-based curriculum, Curriculum 2013 and Kurikulum Merdeka followed each other in sequence. Each arrived with new language, new frameworks, new demands on teachers. The pattern across all of them is the same: redesign moves faster at the top than change moves inside classroom habit. A framework becomes a circular and reaches teachers as another demand added to an already full workload.

The absorption gap as the real failure point

The weakest point in Indonesia's education reform is not the quality of the document. It is the distance between the document and the classroom. Teachers who have not been prepared before launch cannot be expected to absorb new language mid-term. Preparation must precede curriculum, not follow it. Until that sequence changes, the country will keep producing new documents for the same old classroom.

05 — Evidence

Four scenes that ground the argument.

The column builds from observation rather than abstraction — each scene points to where formation succeeds or fails.

EVIDENCE A
The coconut-stall scene

Five young coconuts and two medicinal coconuts. A simple sum. A student reaches for a phone rather than completing the arithmetic mentally. The scene is ordinary. It is also diagnostic: the habit of outsourcing basic calculation has already been formed, and no curriculum change will reach it.

EVIDENCE B
Family as the first site of formation

Attention, patience, recall and self-command first take shape inside family routines — before any school receives the child. The classroom inherits what the family has already built or neglected. This is not an argument against schools; it is an argument about sequence.

EVIDENCE C
Old damage accumulates silently

Weakness visible in secondary school or university has usually been built across earlier years. By the time a teacher sees the deficit, it has been compounding since early childhood. Reform that begins at secondary school or university is already late. The damage is not new; only the visibility is.

EVIDENCE D
Digital outsourcing before school

Early screen exposure normalises handing basic mental work to devices before school has a chance to build stamina. A child accustomed to cognitive outsourcing does not arrive at primary school neutral. They arrive already practised in the habit that school will spend years trying to reverse.

06 — Implications

The repair cannot begin with another launch.

The column maps the wrong moves clearly before naming the right ones — because the pattern of error is as instructive as the prescription.

✗ The Wrong Moves
✗Treat low PISA scores as a score problem — focus on measurement rather than tracing the deficit to its pre-school origin.
✗Launch a new curriculum before teachers are prepared to absorb it — adding language and demand without building readiness.
✗Treat early childhood as a side programme — peripheral to the main business of curriculum reform.
✗Focus on AI detection rules before defining what learning in a field actually requires students to do.
✗Ask universities to repair old habits — expecting the final institution in the sequence to undo what the earliest years built.
✓ The Right Moves
✓Start with cognitive formation — make attention, patience, recall and self-command explicit goals of the earliest years.
✓Give parents practical routines — extend the site of formation beyond school into the household.
✓Rebuild attention, memory and number sense at primary level — protect time for stamina before content completion.
✓Prepare teachers before curriculum launch — continuity and absorption must come before new forms and new circulars.
✓Define what each field still requires students to do — so that AI tools are placed inside a clear account of learning, not beside the absence of one.
© 2026 Dr. Toronata Tambun
07 — Conclusion

Reform must begin before the classroom receives the child.

Three recommendations that follow from the argument — each addressing a different point in the sequence of formation.

1
Treat early childhood as the first site of formation

Make attention, patience, recall and self-command explicit goals of the earliest years — not developmental bonuses, but the foundational work that schooling depends on. This requires policy investment, not rhetoric about the importance of early childhood.

2
Give primary schools room to rebuild core habits

Protect time for reading stamina, memory work and number sense before content completion. A primary school that races to cover curriculum while children cannot yet sustain attention is solving the wrong problem. Sequence matters: habit formation before content delivery.

3
Prepare teachers before curriculum launch

Continuity and absorption must come before new language, new forms and new circulars. Teachers who are not ready before a curriculum is launched cannot be expected to absorb it mid-term. The preparation sequence must be reversed: teacher readiness first, curriculum arrival second.

Closing

"Another launch will only give the country a new document for the same old classroom."
