Indonesia Oil and Gas:
Bureaucracy Reform?

Indonesia's oil decline is not a geology problem but a governance failure — only bureaucracy reform anchored to people's welfare can reverse the trend.

Dr. Toronata Tambun
Director, Yayasan Mens et Manus
© 2026 Dr. Toronata Tambun
01  ·  Introduction

A governance crisis masquerading as a resource shortage.

The numbers tell a story of decline — but the cause is not geological. It is institutional.

Situation

Indonesia's oil production has fallen from 1,437 to under 840 thousand boepd in a decade. Reserves have declined from 5.6 to 3.7 billion barrels since 1992. The R/P ratio stands at just 11.1 years.

Complication

Despite 22 unexplored basins, a world-class PSC framework, and democratic protections for contracts, bureaucratic dysfunction — not geology — is the primary barrier to recovery.

Question

What must Indonesia reform in its governance and regulatory structure to reverse production decline and ensure the resource wealth reaches the people?

Answer

Three-part bureaucracy reform: redefine the regulator as monitor not challenger; revise the Oil and Gas Law for the $100/barrel era; and streamline inter-ministerial decision-making — all for the people's welfare.

© 2026 Dr. Toronata Tambun
02  ·  Research Gap

The opportunity is all there — yet governance determines who wins.

Indonesia's geological endowment is real and waiting. The deficit is not in resources — it is in the regulatory and institutional environment that surrounds them.

Core Gap

Existing policy debate treats Indonesia's oil decline as a supply or pricing problem. It is neither. The gap is governance: a supervisory body that challenges instead of monitors, a legal framework designed for a different era, and ministries that cannot coordinate efficiently. Addressing the geology without addressing the institution is a remedy applied to the wrong problem.

Indonesia competes for investment capital against every other petroleum province in the world. Governance quality is what tips the balance. A country with 22 unexplored basins, a world-invented PSC framework, and democratic contract protections should not struggle to attract capital — yet it does. The gap is internal, not external.

03  ·  Framework

The Governance Imperative: Three Reform Pillars

Bureaucracy reform — not investor attraction — is the master key to unlocking Indonesia's oil and gas future.

"Do the right thing right in the first time is the key for achieving the goal."

— Dr. Toronata Tambun
01
Empower Forensic Auditors

The supervisory body must return to its calling as a monitoring authority — not a challenging one. Competent oil and gas forensic auditors must be empowered to ensure accountability without friction.

02
Revise the Oil and Gas Law

The legal and supply-chain framework must be updated for $100/barrel oil, resource scarcity, and the rising cost of lifting. Adoption of new technology is no longer optional — it must be mandated.

03
Integrate the Ministries

An efficient, lawful inter-ministerial decision process linking Energy, Environment, Manpower, Law, and Administrative Reform ministries is required. Decision delay is policy failure — treat it as one.

04  ·  Details

The Arithmetic of Recovery

Restoring 1,400 thousand boepd in 7 years is mathematically achievable — if the governance conditions are created.

The Scale of the Target

"In 1997–1998, no less than 35 new PSCs were signed in a single round."

Recovering to 1,400 thousand boepd requires roughly 30 new producing fields at 20–35 thousand boepd each. History shows this scale is achievable — it has been done before.

The Pertamina Factor

"Pertamina's domestic effort could contribute 50% of the 7-year recovery target."

PT Pertamina plans to grow from 465,220 to 2.2M boepd in 11 years, with 30% from domestic effort. Government backing of Pertamina is therefore not optional — it is essential strategy.

The Time Imperative

"West Seno went from discovery to production in just 5 years. We must have started yesterday."

The 7-year window is tight but precedented. Every year of delay compounds the production deficit. If the goal is 2021, the starting gun fired in 2014 — and every moment of inaction has a cost.

05  ·  Evidence

The Resource Case: Indonesia's Geology Is Not the Problem

Four data points establish that the geological opportunity is real — and that governance, not resource scarcity, is the binding constraint.

EVIDENCE A
22 Unexplored Basins

IAGE reports 22 basins still unexplored; 15 drilled but hydrocarbon not yet found; 7 with hydrocarbon found but not yet in production. The geological upside is real and waiting.

EVIDENCE B
PSC: Invented in Indonesia

The Production Sharing Contract originated in Indonesia in 1966 and was adopted globally. The framework is sound — negative perceptions stem from misunderstanding cost recovery, not structural flaws.

EVIDENCE C
R/P Ratio of 11.1 Years

Reserves have declined: 5.6 (1992) → 4.7 (2002) → 3.7 (2011) billion barrels. This is a governance-driven metric, not a geological ceiling. The resource is there; the institutional will is absent.

EVIDENCE D
Contract Sanctity Is Protected

UN Resolution 1803 (1962) protects investor rights and requires fair prior compensation for any nationalization. Democratic Indonesia already provides the legal protection investors need.

06  ·  Implications

Diagnosis: The Wrong Remedies Are Already in Play

Policy failure takes two forms: doing the wrong things, and leaving the right things undone. Indonesia is currently doing both.

✗ Wrong Remedy
✗Begging investors to enter at all cost without governance reform
✗Signing new PSCs without fixing the regulatory environment first
✗Dismissing cost recovery out of ignorance of its actual mechanics
✗Nationalizing resources without fair and prior compensation
✗Using oil and gas policy to enrich a group, party, or family
✓ The Right Work
✓Anchor every regulatory framework to people's welfare — unambiguously
✓Redefine the supervisory body as monitor, not challenger or executor
✓Revise the Oil and Gas Law for today's cost and resource environment
✓Build efficient inter-ministerial decision protocols without legal breach
✓Give Pertamina full government support for its domestic production plan
© 2026 Dr. Toronata Tambun
07  ·  Conclusion

Indonesia must do the right things right — for the people, not the powerful.

Three reforms, pursued simultaneously, with people's welfare as the unambiguous north star.

1
Reform the Regulator

Return the supervisory body to monitoring and forensic auditing — not executing or challenging. Empower competent oil and gas auditors to ensure accountability without bureaucratic overreach.

2
Revise the Legal Framework

Update the Oil and Gas Law and supply-chain regulations for $100/barrel oil, resource scarcity, and rising lifting costs. Early adoption of technology must become mandatory — not aspirational.

3
Integrate the Ministries

Create a lawful, efficient inter-ministerial decision system across Energy, Environment, Manpower, Law, and Administrative Reform. Delay in decisions is a policy failure — it must be treated as one.

Closing

If the goal is purely for the benefit of the people, then it is such a noble mission — and must be pursued with utmost effort and supported throughout.
