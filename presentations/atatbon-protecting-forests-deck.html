Atatbon: Protecting forests beyond the harvest

01 · Why This Column Is Different
This is not a commentary on food policy. It is an anthropological reading of a ritual.

Two things now share the name Pesta Babi. One is a controversial YouTube documentary about deforestation, military escorts and land conflict in Papua's south. The other is the ancient pig feast — the Dani call it ebe akho or mawe; the Muyu call it atatbon. Most writing about Papua right now engages the documentary. This column reads the ritual — its objects, what it declares, and what it assumes about the relationship between people and forest.

I · Artefacts
What you can see
The pig raised by name in the forest. The sacred waruk tree. The ceremonial fire and enclosure. Shell money (ot) and exchange stones (je). These are not decorations — they are evidence of years of obligation kept.
II · Espoused Values
What the feast declares
Reciprocity. Ancestral debt honoured. Community witnessed. Mourning ended. Marriages recognized. Adulthood conferred. The feast does not announce abundance — it confirms that continuity of care has been maintained.
III · Underlying Assumption
What the feast takes as given
The forest is not a resource to extract. It is the precondition. You cannot buy the pig shortly before the feast. You cannot buy the forest back after it is gone. The land covenant precedes the harvest by a decade.
02 · The Ritual in Full
The pig is kin. The forest is the contract. The order cannot be shortcut.

03 · Science Confirms What the Feast Already Knew
The Muyu did not need a simulator. They built the asymmetry into the ritual.
Indigenous Knowledge · Atatbon

Atatbon requires the clan to remain connected to the same forest for ten years before the feast is even possible. The pigs cannot be bought. The territory cannot be replaced. Clearing the forest ends the feast — not next year, but for a generation.

En-ROADS Climate Solutions Simulator · Climate Interactive & MIT Sustainability Initiative

Avoiding deforestation and growing new forests operate on fundamentally different time scales. Clearing releases stored carbon immediately. Newly planted trees need years to recover only part of what has been lost. The curves do not meet.

Both point to the same structural fact: the loss is instant; the recovery, measured in decades — if it comes at all.

04 · The Tension
Indonesia must feed its people. That is not in question. Damaging the forest is not an option.

Minister of Agriculture Amran Sulaiman leads with two figures. Citing an FAO report, he says Indonesia ranked second in the world — behind Brazil — for percentage increase in rice production. The Farmer Exchange Rate reached 127, its highest level in 34 years. He has challenged critics to what he calls a data duel. The figures are real and examinable.

Reducing Indonesia's dependence on imported food is a legitimate national goal. It does not conflict with the Muyu principle. What conflicts is the time horizon. The ministry records the current harvest. Muyu families preserve the forest so that the land can continue producing in the years ahead.

Currently Reported
  • Rice production — ranked 2nd globally (FAO)
  • Farmer Exchange Rate — 127, highest in 34 years
Missing from the Report
  • Hectares of intact forest protected from clearing
  • Hectares of degraded land with surviving restored trees
Reporting restoration and clearing separately is not a bureaucratic detail. It prevents restoration from being counted as compensation for a forest that no longer exists.
05 · The Noble Option
Minister Amran can be remembered for both — provider and preserver.
Provider
Rice production ranked second in the world. Farmer Exchange Rate at a 34-year high. Food security delivered at scale.
+
Preserver
The person who returned the blessing to the land — and to the indigenous communities whose forest covenant the harvest depends on.
  1. Food-estate projects must use degraded land first. Intact forest is not available for clearing.
  2. Where clearing is independently justified, restoration is an additional obligation — not a trade-off or offset against what has been lost.
  3. Land may not be declared restored until planted trees have survived for a defined period.
  4. Customary councils — who know the forest boundaries and clan claims — must verify both the area cleared and the survival of the restored forest.
  5. Land permits are renewed only after obligations have been independently verified.
  6. Two new metrics reported separately: hectares of intact forest protected; hectares of degraded land with surviving trees.
06 · Close
Atatbon carries a beauty and wisdom that the documentary's focus on conflict may overshadow.

Beyond the controversy lies a ritual that binds social obligation to the care of the forest across generations, and whose wisdom speaks directly to modern climate action.

Muyu families remain responsible for the same forest throughout the years before a feast can take place. National food policy should require the same continuity — protecting intact forest first, and keeping restoration obligations in force beyond a single harvest or a single ministerial term.
