01  ·  Introduction

This is not a commentary on food policy. It is an anthropological reading of a ritual.

Two things now share the name Pesta Babi. This column reads the ancient one — and what it has to teach national policy.

Two things now share the name Pesta Babi. One is a controversial YouTube documentary about deforestation, military escorts and land conflict in Papua's south. The other is the ancient pig feast: the Dani call it ebe akho or mawe; the Muyu call it atatbon.

Most writing about Papua right now engages the documentary. This column reads the ritual — its objects, what it declares, and what it assumes about the relationship between people and forest.

The Frame

The article does not treat the feast as a cultural ornament. It treats the ritual order as evidence of a forest covenant built over time — and asks what food policy looks like when it inherits that time horizon.

02  ·  Research Gap

The missing time horizon in food policy.

Indonesia's food security debate is measured in harvests. The Muyu measure in decades. That gap in time horizon is the gap this column addresses.

Core Gap

The Ministry of Agriculture reports current harvests. Muyu families preserve the forest so that the land can continue producing in the years ahead. The issue is not food security itself. The issue is the time horizon used to define success. A food policy measured only by the current harvest cannot account for the forest covenant that makes the next harvest possible.

Reducing Indonesia's dependence on imported food is a legitimate national goal. It does not conflict with the Muyu principle. What conflicts is the time horizon. Reporting restoration and clearing in the same column of figures prevents restoration from being held accountable for what it claims to offset.

⚠ The Asymmetry

Clearing releases loss immediately. Newly planted trees need decades to recover only part of what has been removed. The curves do not meet. Policy that treats restoration as compensation for deforestation is built on a false equivalence.

03  ·  Framework

Schein's Cultural Reading: Three Levels of the Atatbon

Objects, declared values, and underlying assumptions carry the argument. The feast operates on all three levels simultaneously.

I
Artefacts
What You Can See
The visible layer

The pig raised by name in the forest. The sacred waruk tree. The ceremonial fire and enclosure. Shell money (ot) and exchange stones (je). These are not decorations — they are evidence of years of obligation kept. Each object is traceable to a decade of forest stewardship that preceded the feast.

II
Espoused Values
What the Feast Declares
The stated layer

Reciprocity. Ancestral debt honoured. Community witnessed. Mourning ended. Marriages recognized. Adulthood conferred. The feast does not announce abundance — it confirms that continuity of care has been maintained. Every declaration in the ritual is a public confirmation of obligations met over years.

III
Underlying Assumption
What the Feast Takes as Given
The foundational layer

The forest is not a resource to extract. It is the precondition. You cannot buy the pig shortly before the feast. You cannot buy the forest back after it is gone. The land covenant precedes the harvest by a decade — and this assumption is so foundational it requires no declaration. It is built into the ritual order itself.

04  ·  Details

The Ten-Year Covenant and the Required Sequence

The pigs cannot be purchased before the feast. There is no shortcut. The order cannot be reversed.

Year 1
Pigs named — raised in forest, not in pens

The covenant begins. Each pig is given a name and raised as kin within ancestral forest territory.

Years 2–4
Learning paths, feeding grounds, sleep patterns

The clan remains connected to their ancestral rivers, ridges and paths. The forest is lived in, not managed from a distance.

Years 5–8
Retaining rivers, ridges and soil boundaries

Ancestral territorial boundaries are held. Any clearing of this forest during this period ends the feast — not next year, but for a generation.

Year 9
Amin bon tibri enters restrictions

Ceremonial preparations begin. The host clan enters a period of obligation and restriction that anticipates the feast.

Year 10
Atatbon — the feast becomes visible to the world

The public settlement of obligations: mourning ends, marriages recognized, young men accepted as adults, debts named, territory boundaries reaffirmed, pigs and exchange objects transferred. The feast is not a spectacle — it is a witnessed accounting.

The Required Sequence

Prepare ceremonial ground → build pig enclosure → plant sacred waruk tree → light ritual fire → amin bon tibri observes restrictions → offerings near waruk → first pig slaughtered before witnesses. Atatbon is not a spectacle. It is a public settlement of obligations accumulated over years. The order cannot be reversed and no step can be purchased.

05  ·  Evidence

Science Confirms What the Feast Already Knew

The Muyu did not need a simulator. They built the asymmetry into the ritual. Two independent knowledge systems arrive at the same structural fact.

EVIDENCE A — Indigenous Knowledge
Atatbon as Ecological Covenant

Atatbon requires the clan to remain connected to the same forest for ten years before the feast is even possible. The pigs cannot be bought. The territory cannot be replaced. Clearing the forest ends the feast — not next year, but for a generation. The ritual encodes an ecological asymmetry that no policy shortcut can override.

EVIDENCE B — Climate Science
En-ROADS Climate Solutions Simulator

Avoiding deforestation and growing new forests operate on fundamentally different time scales. Clearing releases stored carbon immediately. Newly planted trees need years to recover only part of what has been lost. The curves do not meet. Both indigenous knowledge and climate modelling point to the same structural fact: the loss is instant; the recovery, measured in decades — if it comes at all.

EVIDENCE C — Ministry Data
Minister Amran's Data Duel

Minister of Agriculture Amran Sulaiman cites an FAO report placing Indonesia second in the world (behind Brazil) for percentage increase in rice production. The Farmer Exchange Rate reached 127, its highest level in 34 years. These figures are real and examinable — and they do not conflict with the Muyu principle. What conflicts is the time horizon used to define success.

EVIDENCE D — Forest Bond Asymmetry
Preservation Comes Before Restoration

Ten years is not decoration. The ritual requires a decade of continuing obligation before the feast appears. The visible celebration is only the end point. Clearing releases loss immediately. Newly planted trees need decades to recover only part of what has been removed. Policy must protect intact forests first — restoration cannot be counted as compensation for avoidable destruction of functioning ecosystems.

06  ·  Implications

What Is Measured and What Is Missing

A food policy metric must not stop at the current harvest. Reporting restoration and clearing separately prevents restoration from being counted as compensation for forest that no longer exists.

✓Farmer Exchange Rate, 127 — highest in 34 years
⚠ System Continuity — Missing from the Report
⚠Hectares of intact forest protected from clearing
⚠Hectares of degraded land with surviving restored trees

"Indonesia must feed its people. That is not in question. Damaging the forest is not an option."

— The tension the column holds simultaneously

The ministry records the current harvest. Muyu families preserve the forest so that the land can continue producing in the years ahead. These two orientations are not inherently opposed — but they become opposed when food-estate projects treat intact forest as available land for clearing.

07  ·  Conclusion

The Noble Option: Provider and Preserver

Minister Amran can be remembered for both. Six policy commitments define what that dual legacy requires.

Provider

Rice production ranked second in the world. Farmer Exchange Rate at a 34-year high. Food security delivered at scale. This achievement is real and must be held.

Preserver

The person who returned the blessing to the land — and to the Indigenous communities whose forest covenant the harvest depends on. The larger legacy is to keep the land capable of feeding future generations.

1

Food-estate projects must use degraded land first. Intact forest is not available for clearing.

2

Where clearing is independently justified, restoration is an additional obligation — not a trade-off or offset against what has been lost.

3

Land may not be declared restored until planted trees have survived for a defined period.

4

Customary councils — who know the forest boundaries and clan claims — must verify both the area cleared and the survival of the restored forest.

5

Land permits are renewed only after obligations have been independently verified.

6

Two new metrics reported separately: hectares of intact forest protected; hectares of degraded land with surviving trees.

Closing

Muyu families remain responsible for the same forest throughout the years before a feast can take place. National food policy should require the same continuity — protecting intact forest first, and keeping restoration obligations in force beyond a single harvest or a single ministerial term. The forest covenant must outlast the harvest.
